Why Apex Aviation

Learn it in the sim.
Master it in the airplane.

Traditional flight training puts students in the aircraft to learn — at $145/hr, in real weather, with no rewind button. Apex reverses that model. Every maneuver, procedure, and emergency scenario is introduced in the AATD simulator first. Students learn under controlled conditions, repeat without burning fuel, and debrief after every pass. By the time they step into the aircraft, they already know exactly what to do.

The result: faster progress, higher retention, lower total cost — and pilots who arrive at the checkride genuinely prepared.

  • Simulator-first teaching — aircraft is where you apply it
  • AATD-certified simulator with FAA-credited hours
  • Flexible scheduling — no weather cancellations in the sim
  • Scenario-based from day one — proficiency, not just currency

The Team

Built by a pilot
who needed this training.

Andrew Serrazina founded Apex because the training he wanted as an active IFR pilot didn't exist in Austin — focused, scenario-based recurrent work without the student-pilot machinery around it.

Andrew Serrazina

Founder & Chief Instructor

Andrew's view is simple: most instrument pilots are not as current as they think they are, and most recurrent training is too easy to actually change that. He built Apex because he wanted a place in Austin where a working IFR pilot could get an honest assessment, a hard scenario, and a debrief that didn't pull punches — without having to wade through a flight school structured for private pilot students. Every aspect of the Apex curriculum reflects what he would want as the pilot in the left seat.

Ground school and checkride prep sessions are conducted via video call. Andrew shares his screen for diagrams, charts, and ACS task walkthroughs. Sessions run on a fixed weekly schedule — Monday and Wednesday for Private Pilot, Tuesday for Instrument Rating, Thursday for Commercial. Checkride prep is scheduled individually.

Yes. The Apex AATD is FAA-certified, which means an IPC conducted in the simulator with a CFII satisfies the requirements of FAR 61.57(d). Approaches flown in an AATD also count toward the 6-approach currency requirement under FAR 61.57(c). The simulator opens in Austin in January 2027.

Currency means you meet the FAA's minimums to act as PIC. Proficiency means you can actually handle what real IMC throws at you. A pilot can be legally current after six approaches in a simulator on a clear day and still be dangerously unprepared for a partial-panel situation in actual conditions. Apex trains for proficiency — currency is a byproduct.
