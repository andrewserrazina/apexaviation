Sim hours that count
toward your certificate.

The Apex Redbird FMX is FAA-qualified as an AATD — which means hours logged in the simulator count directly toward your rating requirements. The numbers below are the FAA maximums under 14 CFR Part 61.

Private Pilot Certificate
2.5
Hours AATD Credit
Up to 2.5 of the required 40 total flight hours may be logged in an AATD under Part 61 — but the real value is what you learn before those aircraft hours start.
14 CFR §61.109(k)
0 hrs2.5 of 40 hrs
What this means: Every maneuver and procedure introduced in the sim first. By the time you're in the airplane, you've already done it — which means faster progress on those 40 hours.
Instrument Rating
20
Hours AATD Credit
Up to 20 of the 40 required instrument flight hours may be logged in an AATD. That's half your instrument time — at $90/hr instead of aircraft rates. The math is significant.
14 CFR §61.65(i)
0 hrs20 of 40 hrs instrument time
What this means: 20 hours of ILS, RNAV, holds, partial panel, and missed approaches — logged, FAA-credited, and debriefed. Before you ever file IFR in actual IMC.
Commercial Pilot Certificate
50
Hours AATD Credit
Up to 50 hours in an AATD may be applied toward the total aeronautical experience required for a commercial certificate. At $90/hr versus aircraft wet rates, this materially reduces your total training cost.
14 CFR §61.129(i)
0 hrs50 of 250 hrs total time
What this means: 50 hours of commercial maneuver polish, scenario work, and systems mastery — logged and counted — before you start burning expensive aircraft time on the same material.

The Apex Method

Learn it in the sim.
Apply it in the airplane.

1
Introduce in the AATD
Every new maneuver and procedure is introduced in the simulator first. No pressure. No cost per mistake. Full instructor attention on the learning, not the flying.
2
Repeat until automatic
Reset and run it again. Partial panel missed approach — ten times — until the scan is a reflex. You can't do this in a $145/hr aircraft. You can here.
3
Debrief with data
Every session debriefed with flight track replay. You see exactly where the deviation started, when the correction came, and what the profile looked like from outside the cockpit.
4
Confirm in the aircraft
By the time you climb into the airplane, you've already done the maneuver. The aircraft is where you prove it — not where you learn it. That's the fundamental difference.
