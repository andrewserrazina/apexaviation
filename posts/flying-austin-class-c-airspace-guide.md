If you fly out of any of the general aviation airports in the Austin metro — KEDC, KGTU, KHYI, 50R, T74 — you're operating in or adjacent to one of the fastest-growing Class C airspace environments in the country.

Austin-Bergstrom International Airport (KAUS) handles over 20 million passengers a year and the traffic volume shows it. Southwest, American, Delta, United, and a growing list of international carriers all flow through a single Class C shelf that affects every GA pilot within 20 nautical miles.

Flying that airspace well is not complicated — but it requires knowing the rules, building a mental model of the system, and developing the radio fluency to communicate efficiently with approach control. Here's what Central Texas pilots need to know.

## The Structure of Austin's Class C

Austin Class C consists of two layers centered on KAUS:

- **Inner ring (0–4,000 ft MSL):** From the surface up to 4,000 feet MSL, extending 5 nautical miles from the airport
- **Outer ring (1,200–4,000 ft MSL):** From 1,200 feet AGL up to 4,000 feet MSL, extending from 5 to 10 nautical miles

You must establish **two-way radio communication** with Austin Approach before entering either ring. Unlike Class B, you don't need an explicit "cleared into the Class C" — you need ATC to acknowledge your call sign. If they say "N12345, standby," you're not in — wait until they give you a full readback.

Above 4,000 feet MSL and within 10-20 NM, you're in the Class C outer area — not technically part of the Class C, but ATC services are still available and frequently used.

## The Airports Around the Shelf

Understanding which airports sit inside, underneath, or adjacent to the Class C changes how you plan every flight.

**Austin Executive Airport (KEDC)** — Georgetown sector, northeast of KAUS. Sits outside the Class C rings but within the outer area. Departures heading south toward KAUS will quickly enter Class C airspace; departures heading north toward Georgetown or beyond are generally clear. KEDC is the most popular GA airport in the metro and has its own active pattern — situational awareness matters here.

**Georgetown Municipal (KGTU)** — North of the Class C, well clear. Relaxed environment for training, with a VOR that's useful for instrument work. One of the best places in the metro to shoot approaches without fighting traffic.

**San Marcos Regional (KHYI)** — South of KAUS, outside the Class C rings. ILS-equipped, towered, and far enough south to offer a clean IFR environment without the KAUS traffic load. This is where Apex's training aircraft is based.

**Lago Vista (RR6)** / **Burnet (BMQ)** — Northwest, well clear of Class C. Scenic Hill Country routes, good for cross-country building, but require awareness of terrain and the weather patterns discussed below.

## Radio Work That Actually Works

The single biggest thing that separates pilots who enjoy flying through Austin's Class C from those who dread it is radio fluency. ATC is busy. They appreciate crisp, accurate, predictable transmissions.

Before calling Austin Approach, have ready:
- Your position (relative to KAUS — bearing and distance, or relative to a known fix)
- Your altitude
- Your destination or intended transit direction
- Your transponder code (squawk 1200 for VFR if not yet assigned)

**Example initial call:**
*"Austin Approach, Cessna 172 N4521X, ten miles northeast, three thousand five hundred, request Class Charlie transition, southbound."*

ATC will either give you a squawk code and traffic advisories, or tell you to standby. Either way, you've established communication and can proceed accordingly once they acknowledge your call sign.

If you're departing a Class C airport on an IFR clearance, expect a void time or a "release" from departure control before you can go. Know this in advance and have it in your notes.

## The Frequency Split

Austin Approach/Departure uses multiple sectors depending on your position:

- **North/Northeast sector:** 124.9
- **South/Southwest sector:** 127.15
- **East sector:** 135.9

These can change, and ATC will hand you off as you transit. Have your radio ready for a frequency change mid-transit — this is normal and not a problem if you're expecting it.

Check the current frequencies on your chart or in ForeFlight before every flight. Pilot/Controller Glossary aside, the practical rule is: if you're unsure which sector, call 124.9 first — it's the most commonly used general frequency for the north side of KAUS.

## Special Flight Rules and Temporary Restrictions

Austin hosts major events — Formula 1 at COTA, University of Texas games, political visits — that trigger TFRs over and around the city. These are not small. COTA TFRs can push 10-15 NM radius and stack up to 17,000 feet.

Make checking TFRs part of your preflight for every flight in this airspace, not just international travel. The FAA's official TFR feed at tfr.faa.gov is authoritative; ForeFlight pulls it automatically, but always double-check.

## Building Confidence in the System

The pilots who are most comfortable in Class C airspace are the ones who've flown through it enough times that the radio calls are reflexive and the mental picture of the shelf is automatic.

If you're still building that confidence, two things accelerate it faster than anything else: simulator training that puts you in ATC communication scenarios repeatedly without the task saturation of actually flying, and deliberate practice transiting the airspace VFR before you try to do it IFR.
