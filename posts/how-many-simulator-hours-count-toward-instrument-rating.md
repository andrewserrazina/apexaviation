One of the most common questions we get from instrument students is simple: **how much of my training can happen in the simulator?**

The short answer is more than most pilots realize.

## The FAA Rule: Part 61.65(i)

Under **14 CFR §61.65(i)**, a pilot training toward an instrument rating under Part 61 may log up to **20 hours** of flight training time in an Aviation Training Device (ATD) — provided that device meets the FAA's AATD (Advanced Aviation Training Device) qualification standard.

That's out of the 40 total instrument flight hours required (15 of which must be with an authorized instructor). So in a well-structured program, **half of your instrument flight training can happen in the sim**.

## What Qualifies as an AATD?

Not every sim counts. A basic home flight simulator doesn't qualify. An AATD is a specific category of FAA-approved device that meets defined fidelity standards for avionics, flight model, and system simulation.

Apex Aviation's Redbird FMX is an FAA-qualified AATD — meaning every hour you fly in it counts toward your 20-hour allowance, provided it's logged with an authorized instructor and covers the appropriate training tasks.

## Why This Matters for Your Training

The financial math is straightforward. Aircraft time runs significantly more per hour than simulator time. If you're paying $145/hr for the aircraft and $90/hr for the AATD, substituting 20 hours of aircraft time with 20 hours of sim time saves real money without sacrificing — and arguably improving — the quality of your training.

More importantly, the simulator lets you do things you can't safely do in an actual aircraft:

- **Pause and reset** at the moment of a mistake
- **Repeat an approach** until it's automatic
- **Fail systems** without risk — vacuum pump, gyros, pitot heat
- **Practice in actual IMC** without needing actual IMC

Students who use AATD time well arrive at the aircraft phase with stronger scan habits, better situational awareness, and more confident radio technique. The aircraft becomes the environment where they prove the skills they've already built — not where they're learning them from scratch.

Before a student flies their first instrument hour in the CTSW, they've already built their scan, flown multiple approaches, and handled abnormal procedures in the AATD. The aircraft hours that follow are more efficient because the cognitive foundation is already in place.

If you're pursuing your instrument rating and you're not using AATD time to its full legal allowance, you're leaving money on the table and making your training harder than it needs to be.
