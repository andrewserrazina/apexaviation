IFR Currency · 5 min read

How Austin Pilots Stay
IFR Current.

Central Texas weather is beautiful — which is exactly the problem for pilots trying to stay instrument current.

The Currency Trap

Austin gets roughly 300 days of flyable VFR weather per year. If you're a GA pilot based at Austin Executive, Georgetown, or San Marcos, you can go months without ever needing your instrument rating. The skies cooperate, you fly VFR, and everything feels fine — until you check your logbook and realize it's been seven months since you flew an approach.

This is the currency trap: conditions that are too good. Instrument currency doesn't decay dramatically in a single week. It seeps away quietly, approach by approach, hold by hold, until the skills that felt automatic start requiring deliberate effort. By the time you notice the slippage, you're usually already out of currency — and often further from proficiency than the legal threshold suggests.

What the 66 HIT Rule Actually Requires

Under 14 CFR 61.57(c), to act as PIC under IFR or in weather below VFR minimums, you must have logged within the preceding six months: six instrument approaches, holding procedures, and intercepting and tracking courses through the use of navigational electronic systems. That's the "66 HIT" shorthand — six approaches, six months, plus holds and course interception.

There's also an important nuance in the regulation: those approaches can be logged in actual IMC, simulated IMC (under a view-limiting device), or in a flight training device or AATD with a qualified instructor. The FAA gives you multiple legitimate paths to stay current — which means there's no good reason to let the six-month clock run out.

If you do let it lapse, the path back is an Instrument Proficiency Check with a CFII. That's not a punishment — it's the regulation's way of saying that skills this perishable deserve a structured reset, not a self-assessment.

Currency Is Not Proficiency

This distinction matters more than most pilots acknowledge. Currency is a legal threshold — a minimum. Proficiency is a performance standard — what it actually takes to fly safely in IMC. They're not the same, and the gap between them can be significant.

Consider: you can maintain currency by logging six practice RNAV approaches on a clear night under the hood with a safety pilot. You never touch a cloud, you never deal with ATC workload, you never manage the situational awareness demands of actual IMC. You're technically current. But if you then accept a clearance into Austin on a night with a 400-foot OVC and visibility dropping to a mile, the approach that was routine in CAVU is now a very different animal.

Proficiency means your instrument scan is automatic under workload. It means you can brief an approach, manage communications, and configure the aircraft without any one task crowding out the others. It means you've practiced missed approaches enough that the execution is reflexive, not effortful. Currency doesn't guarantee any of that — it just tells you how long ago you flew six approaches.

Practical Strategies for Staying Current in Austin

The most efficient path to real currency is to use your AATD sessions for approaches, not just ground study. An FAA-approved Advanced Aviation Training Device, flown with a CFII, counts toward the 66 HIT requirement under 61.57(c)(2). You can knock out six approaches in an hour, fly three different approach types, shoot a hold, and practice a partial-panel missed approach — all without burning avgas, scheduling around weather, or dealing with actual controller workload when you're already rusty.

A second strategy: file IFR on local flights even in VMC. Austin has Class C airspace with radar coverage and controllers who can give you practice approaches all day. Filing IFR on a clear-day cross-country to San Antonio or Waco keeps you in the system — working with ATC, flying the procedures, briefing approaches before you need them. The approaches count toward your currency, and you arrive more proficient than you left.

A third option, especially useful if you don't fly often enough to build natural currency: find a safety pilot partner. Under 61.57(c)(1), approaches flown under simulated IMC with a safety pilot count. Two pilots who are both trying to stay current can trade off — one flies, one safety-pilots — and both benefit from the debrief afterward. It costs nothing but fuel and a shared schedule.

The 90-Day Creep

Here's the pattern I see regularly: a pilot finishes their last IFR flight in, say, October. November is beautiful VFR weather and a busy work schedule. December is the holidays. By January, it's been three months — but three months feels recent, so they tell themselves they'll fly IFR next month. February and March pass. In April, they check the calendar and realize they're almost out of the six-month window.

At that point, some pilots hustle to log six approaches in a hurry — which is exactly the wrong approach to currency. Rushed currency-building under pressure is stress-flying, not proficiency-building. You're checking boxes, not rebuilding skills.

The fix is simple: don't let the three-month mark pass without at least a simulator session. Treat two or three approaches every two months as maintenance, the same way you'd treat an oil change — something you do on a schedule before you need it, not because you noticed a problem.
