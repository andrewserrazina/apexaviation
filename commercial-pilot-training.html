Commercial Pilot Training in Austin

The commercial certificate isn't just a license to get paid — it's proof you can fly with precision and judgment under pressure. We train to that standard, using every FAA-approved simulator hour to get you there efficiently.

How Apex Trains Commercial Pilots

At the commercial level, precision matters. Steep turns, chandelles, lazy eights, and emergency procedures all have performance standards — and the fastest way to meet those standards is to build the muscle memory first in the simulator, where you can reset and repeat without time pressure or cost. Apex uses the AATD to develop procedural mastery before any commercial maneuver is attempted in the aircraft. Flight training takes place in a FlightDesign CTSW based at San Marcos Regional Airport (KHYI).

The result is that aircraft hours are productive from the first session — not spent on fundamentals that should have been locked in on the ground. With up to 50 AATD hours credited toward the 250-hour total under §61.129(i), the simulator-first approach also keeps your total training cost significantly lower than a traditional program.

FAA Requirements for the Commercial Pilot Certificate

Under FAA Part 61, the commercial certificate requires 250 total hours of flight time — of which up to 50 hours may be completed in an AATD under §61.129(i). Those 250 hours must include 100 hours of PIC time, 50 hours of PIC cross-country, 10 hours of instrument time, and 10 hours in a complex or TAA aircraft. You'll also need a night cross-country of at least 100 nm with 10 takeoffs and landings, a day cross-country of 300 nm with at least three stops, and you must pass the Commercial Pilot (CAX) written exam before your checkride.

Who This Is For

For career changers entering aviation, the commercial certificate is the credential that makes the transition official. Whether you're targeting charter, corporate, or an airline career path, you need 250 hours that stand behind a certificate — and a program that actually prepares you for professional-level flying, not just time-building.

Many private pilots pursue the commercial simply to keep flying with purpose. Building hours toward a goal — with structured instruction and measurable progress — beats renting an aircraft and flying solo patterns indefinitely. The commercial adds precision maneuvers, IFR rigor, and a higher standard of aeronautical decision-making to everything you already know.

If you're planning to instruct — CFI is a natural next step — the commercial certificate is a prerequisite. At Apex, commercial students train under the same scenario-based methodology as everyone else. We're not interested in hour-building for its own sake. Every session has a purpose, and you'll leave every flight knowing exactly what you improved and what's next.
