What to Expect From
an IPC in Austin.

An Instrument Proficiency Check isn't a test. It's a diagnostic — and if it's easy, something is wrong.

What the FAA Actually Requires

The IPC is governed by 14 CFR 61.57(d) and conducted against the Instrument Rating Airman Certification Standards. Your CFII must observe you accomplish tasks from six ACS areas: preflight planning and procedures, instrument flight, instrument approach procedures, emergency operations, postflight procedures, and cross-country flight operations. That's the floor — not the ceiling.

The ACS tasks aren't arbitrary. They're a structured way to verify that you can actually operate in the system: file and fly an IFR flight plan, brief and fly multiple approach types (precision, non-precision, approach with vertical guidance), execute a missed approach, and handle partial-panel and abnormal situations. Notice what's not on that list: demonstrating that you can still squeeze into a 200-foot ceiling when you haven't flown IFR in eight months.

Legal vs. Useful

Here's the honest version of what "passed your IPC" means: a CFII signed off that they observed you accomplish the required tasks. It does not mean you're ready to fly a coupled approach into Austin Bergstrom in actual IMC with your family in the back seats. Those are not the same thing, and conflating them is how pilots get into trouble.

The IPC is most useful when treated as a structured debrief of where you actually are — not a hurdle to clear. A good IPC will expose things you didn't know were slipping. That's the point. If you walk out of an IPC without learning anything, one of two things is true: either you're remarkably proficient, or the check wasn't probing hard enough.

What I Look For

When I run an IPC, I'm watching things the ACS doesn't grade directly. Instrument scan quality is the first tell. A pilot who hasn't flown IFR in a while will often revert to fixation — staring at the altimeter while the heading wanders, then chasing the heading while the altitude unwinds. The scan should be automatic, a rhythm that doesn't require conscious thought. If you're thinking about scanning, the scan isn't there yet.

Missed approach decision-making is the second thing I focus on. Most pilots can fly an ILS to minimums. The real question is what happens at decision altitude when the runway isn't there: is the go-around immediate and decisive, or is there a half-second of hesitation while the brain negotiates? In actual IMC, that pause costs you altitude you don't have. I want to see the power go in and the pitch come up before the "missed" call is even finished.

Partial panel work reveals how deeply the procedures are internalized. When I cover the attitude indicator and heading indicator, a lot of pilots who flew fine five minutes ago suddenly become uncertain about what they're looking at and what it means. Partial panel flying isn't about raw aircraft control — it's about knowing your alternate reference sources cold enough that the loss of primary instruments doesn't break your mental model of the flight.

I also pay attention to how you brief approaches. A pilot who can recite the minimums and missed approach instructions without hunting through the chart is a pilot who has done this enough that it's reflexive. Someone who has to work hard to find the information is someone whose approach flying is mentally expensive — and that cost shows up when conditions get harder.

How to Prepare

Show up having reviewed the charts for the approaches we'll fly. You should be able to brief each approach from memory: frequencies, final approach course, decision altitude or MDA, missed approach procedure, field elevation. If you need the plate in front of you to do that, fine — but know where to look and what you're looking for before we strap in.

Review your aircraft systems with an emphasis on the avionics. Know how your GPS enters and executes a missed approach. Know how to fly your autopilot into a hold and then hand-fly out of it. Know what your AHRS failure looks like on the G1000 or Garmin panel, and what your backup instruments are. These aren't gotcha questions — they're things you should know before you fly in IMC regardless of whether there's a CFII watching.

If you've been out of the IFR system for more than six months, consider doing a ground session or a simulator session before we fly. Not because I expect you to be perfect, but because the first session after a long gap is often spent remembering rather than refining. Coming in with the procedures already reactivated means we can spend the flight actually measuring proficiency instead of rebuilding it from scratch.

What a Good Debrief Looks Like

A checkbox debrief sounds like this: "Looked good, sign here." A real debrief identifies the two or three things that were actually weak and gives you a specific practice plan. Maybe your scan broke down under workload on the ILS but held together on the RNAV. Maybe your missed approach was solid but your hold entry geometry was off. Those are actionable findings — and they're the reason the IPC is worth doing.

I keep notes during every IPC and go through them point by point at the end. If something was genuinely good, I'll say so. If something needs work, I'll tell you what I saw, why it matters, and what to do about it. You should leave the session knowing exactly where you stand and exactly what to practice before you fly IFR in actual conditions again.
