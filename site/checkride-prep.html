What's my DPE actually
going to ask me?

There's no official list — and there shouldn't be, since a real oral exam is a scenario-based conversation, not a fixed script. This free guide walks through 10 of the questions that come up again and again, why DPEs ask each one, and a specific tip for how to actually land it on exam day.

Free Guide Preview — Question 7 of 10
Oral Exam Question
What are the "four C's" of lost procedures?
Model Answer
Climb (for a better view and radio/GPS reception), Communicate (contact ATC or Flight Service for help), Confess (clearly state you're unsure of your position), and Comply (follow the guidance you're given).
Why DPEs Ask This
This question tests whether you'd actually use the process in a real moment of uncertainty — including the psychologically harder step of admitting you're lost to ATC.
Checkride Success Tip
There's no professional downside to confessing uncertainty to a controller. They deal with this constantly and are there to help, not to judge — say this with genuine confidence.

Why Students Struggle

Most students don't fail because
they didn't study.

They fail because they weren't ready
to explain their decisions.

The private pilot oral exam is not a trivia test. A DPE wants to know whether you understand the material well enough to apply it in real-world situations. That means you need more than memorized answers — you need structure, confidence, and practical judgment.

💬

You know the acronym, but freeze when asked "why."

Memorizing ARROW or PAVE isn't enough. The DPE wants to hear you reason through it — not recite it.

📋

You studied the regulations, but struggle to apply them to scenarios.

Knowing a rule and being able to use it in a real situation are two different skills. Oral exams test the second one.

🎙

You feel prepared at home, but nervous when answering out loud.

Confidence under pressure doesn't come from reading. It comes from practicing the actual format of the oral exam.

Sample — Module 03: Aircraft Airworthiness
Oral Exam Question
What makes an aircraft airworthy?
Model Answer
An aircraft is airworthy when it conforms to its type certificate or approved alterations and is in a condition for safe operation. Both conditions must be true — conformity alone isn't enough, and neither is a recent inspection.
Common Student Mistake
Defining airworthiness only as "having a current annual inspection." This misses the two-part legal standard entirely.
What the DPE Is Evaluating
Whether you understand airworthiness as a two-part legal and practical standard — not just a maintenance status.
Real-World Application
As PIC, you are responsible for determining that the aircraft is both legally conforming and physically safe before every flight. Both parts of the standard are your responsibility.
