Why This Matters

Recognizing a spin early is the difference
between a non-event and an emergency.

Most pilots never actually see a spin develop before it happens to them. By the time it's obvious, you're already past the easiest phase to recover. Knowing the stages — and reacting correctly the moment you recognize one — is what keeps this a training topic instead of an accident report.

⚠️

It starts with an uncoordinated stall.

Excessive rudder or a skidding turn during an already-stalled wing is almost always where it begins — often without much warning.

🔄

The incipient phase is your best chance.

Recovery gets harder the longer a spin develops. The earliest phase — right as rotation begins — is the easiest to stop.

🛬

Base-to-final is where it gets dangerous.

An overshoot plus rudder input in the pattern means a spin with very little altitude to recover in — the single most dangerous version of this scenario.

The Stages
of a Spin.

Recognize it early. Recover correctly.

⚠️

Entry

  • Stall is initiated
  • Yaw/rudder is introduced
  • Aircraft departs controlled flight

This is where control is lost.

🔄

Incipient

  • Rotation begins
  • Noticable wing drop
  • Transitioning horizontal to vertical speed

Easiest phase to recover.

🌀

Developed

  • Stable rotation
  • Constant rate of turn + descent
  • Fully stalled condition

Fully developed spin condition.

✔️

Recovery

  • Correct recovery inputs applied
  • Rotation stops
  • Wings regain lift

Back to controlled flight.

Why Spins Happen

Uncoordinated stall, excessive rudder, skidding base-to-final — the same three root causes, every time.

Common Mistakes

Using ailerons, not enough rudder, and hesitating are the three most common ways a recovery attempt goes wrong.

The Most Dangerous Scenario

Base-to-final overshoot plus rudder input equals a spin at low altitude — the scenario with the least margin to recover.
