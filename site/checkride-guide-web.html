The Regulation
14 CFR 91.21314 CFR 91.205

Landing lights are not required equipment for daytime VFR flight under 14 CFR 91.205. However, you can't simply ignore an inoperative instrument or piece of equipment. You need to go through the proper process.

The MEL Process (14 CFR 91.213): First, check whether the aircraft has an FAA-approved Minimum Equipment List (MEL). If it does, defer the item per the MEL and its associated procedures. If there's no MEL, check: is this equipment listed in 14 CFR 91.205, required by airworthiness directive, or required by the aircraft's type certificate data sheet? If not, you can placard it "inoperative" and fly — provided the inoperative item doesn't compromise airworthiness.
Pro Tip

Say the process out loud in order: "MEL → 91.205 / type cert / AD → placard-and-fly or ground-and-fix." DPEs want to see the process, not just the destination.

Compensation and Private Pilot Privileges
14 CFR 61.113

Private pilots may not act as PIC for compensation or hire. However, there is an exception for pro rata share of operating expenses.

The key question is: do you and your friend share a common purpose for this flight? If you were already planning to take this exact flight, and your friend is coming along and sharing the cost — including your own proportionate share — that is a pro rata cost share with a common purpose, which is legal.

If your friend is essentially paying you to fly them somewhere you wouldn't otherwise be going — that's compensation, and it requires at least a commercial certificate.
Pro Tip

The phrase the DPE wants to hear: "pro rata share with a common purpose." Define both parts. If only one is present, it likely doesn't qualify.

Risk Management, Not Just Checklist
The wrong answer here is a reflexive "drain more sumps until it clears." The right answer shows reasoning and risk management awareness.

You drain additional samples from every sump, note how much water you're finding, and assess whether the volume and location is consistent with condensation contamination or something more serious (fuel truck contamination, tank breach, etc.).

If water continues to appear, or appears in unexpected locations, you do not fly — you ground the aircraft and contact maintenance. No flight is worth a fuel-starved engine. You also have a duty to inform other pilots and the FBO.
Pro Tip

Narrate your reasoning, not just your actions: "I want to understand what I'm seeing before I make a go/no-go decision." That's what DPEs are evaluating.

Battery, Load Shedding, Time Management
An alternator failure means the battery is now your only electrical source — and it's draining. This is an abnormal procedure, not an emergency, but it becomes an emergency if you don't manage it well.

Immediate priorities:
  1. Verify — check the ammeter, alternator circuit breaker, and alternator switch (might be a nuisance trip)
  2. Reduce electrical load immediately — non-essential avionics, lights, and equipment off
  3. Estimate remaining battery life — most GA batteries give 30–60 minutes depending on load
  4. Navigate toward the nearest suitable airport with ATC assistance (squawk 7600 if comms fail)
  5. Declare emergency if warranted
Night flight changes the math significantly — you need landing and position lights, which accelerates battery drain.
Pro Tip

"An alternator failure gives me time to think — I'm going to use it, not panic through it." This sentence communicates pilot-in-command mindset under pressure.

Clearance Isn't Implied
14 CFR 91.131

No. "Standby" is not a clearance to enter Class Bravo. Class B airspace requires an explicit, specific clearance to enter — "cleared into the Bravo" — not an acknowledgment of your request.

At three miles out, you need to take positive action: turn away from the Bravo, descend below it, or hold outside until you receive a clearance. Proceeding inbound while "standing by" is a pilot deviation waiting to happen.

If ATC never gets back to you: remain outside the Bravo and try again on guard or a different frequency if necessary.
Pro Tip

Don't just say "I can't enter." Describe your avoidance plan — "I'd turn X degrees, descend below the shelf, and re-request." DPEs want to see the action, not just the rule.

ARROW — What Each Document Proves
14 CFR 91.914 CFR 91.203

The mnemonic is ARROW. But the advanced answer isn't just knowing the acronym — it's knowing what each document proves about the aircraft's airworthiness and legal status.

A — Airworthiness Certificate: Proves the FAA found this aircraft to be airworthy at time of manufacture. It's permanent unless revoked.

R — Registration: Proves legal ownership and US registration. Expires every 3 years — check the date.

R — Radio Station License: Required when flying internationally (FCC). Optional domestically.

O — Operating Handbook (AFM/POH): FAA-approved operating limitations are in here — Vne, weight limits, required equipment.

W — Weight and Balance: Current W&B data must be onboard and accurate for this configuration.
Pro Tip

For each document say: "This document proves ___." Not just what it is — what it means for the legal and safe operation of the aircraft. That framing separates prepared applicants from memorizers.

Angle of Attack — Not Airspeed
Yes. A stall is an angle-of-attack event, not an airspeed event.

When the wing's critical angle of attack is exceeded, airflow over the wing separates and lift collapses — regardless of how fast the airplane is moving. This is why accelerated stalls (occurring at higher-than-normal airspeeds due to load factor) are possible and why they're more dangerous — they happen faster and with less warning.

Load factor increases stall speed. In a 60° banked turn, you're pulling 2Gs — stall speed increases by approximately 41%. At cruise airspeed, if you pull hard enough to exceed critical AoA, you will stall.
Pro Tip

Open with the punchline: "A stall is an angle-of-attack event, not an airspeed event." Then explain why. DPEs respect concise, accurate lead answers followed by supporting reasoning.

Continuous Reassessment and Margin of Safety
A convective SIGMET is not a soft advisory. It's an urgent weather warning issued for severe convective activity — thunderstorms, hail, tornadoes, or embedded convection. A private pilot flying VFR has no business being near a convective SIGMET area unless they have a clear, wide route deviation plan.

The smart answer here isn't just to go or not go — it's to demonstrate the mindset of a weather-aware pilot: check current satellite and radar, not just METARs; look at where the cells are moving; and if there's any doubt, don't go.

If you do fly, maintain a 20 nautical mile lateral berth from any visible convective activity. Radar and visual aren't always consistent — the VFR cloud clearances don't apply to thunderstorms.
Pro Tip

Show the DPE your margin of safety: "I want 20 miles between me and any cell, and I'd rather land short and wait than push through." Decisive conservatism is a pass. Wishful thinking is a fail.

The 4 C's
Getting temporarily unsure of your position is not an emergency — refusing to acknowledge it is what makes it one. Use the 4 C's:

Climb — Better visibility, better radio coverage, more options. Buy time and altitude.

Communicate — Contact ATC on 121.5 MHz (guard) or the nearest FSS. Radar contact gives you an instant position fix.

Confess — Tell ATC you're unsure of your position. This is not a career-ending admission. It's what safe pilots do.

Comply — Follow ATC instructions once you're in contact. They do this all day.
Pro Tip

"There's no professional downside to telling ATC I'm unsure of my position." Frame it proactively — it shows you understand the system and have zero ego about using it. That's what DPEs want to see.

Personal Minimums vs. Legal Floor
Legal minimums are the floor — not the standard. A private pilot with 65 hours has no business flying in conditions that challenge instrument-rated pilots.

The answer the DPE wants to hear is that you have real, specific personal minimums — and that you understand why they exist above the legal floor.

A strong applicant can name their personal minimums: "I don't fly VFR below 2,000 feet AGL or 5 miles visibility until I have more experience in marginal conditions — ideally with an instructor first."

This isn't timidity. It's aeronautical decision-making. The DPE wants to know you'll still be flying in a year — and that you understand the difference between can and should.
Pro Tip

Have a real, specific number ready. Not "I'd probably wait" — give them your ceiling and visibility floor. Specificity signals that you've actually thought this through, not just rehearsed the right sounding answer.

Challenge Scenarios

These questions have no clean, single answer. They're designed to stress-test your decision-making process — the same way a DPE will.

1
Your alternator fails one hour into a night cross-country. You've shed all non-essential load. How long do you have, and what are your priorities in order?
Think about night-specific electrical loads you can't shed — and what that does to your timeline.
2
A friend offers to pay you $200 to fly them to a meeting in a city you weren't planning to visit. Can you accept as a private pilot?
Contrast this with the Generous Friend scenario — what's different, and why does it matter?
3
Your Knowledge Test showed two missed questions in the airspace category. The DPE asks what you did to address those weak areas before today. What do you say?
The DPE isn't looking for a perfect score — they're looking for evidence of self-awareness and continued learning.
4
The runway is 2,200 feet long. Your calculated takeoff distance is 1,850 feet. It's a hot afternoon with a light tailwind. Do you go?
The numbers technically work. Walk through density altitude, tailwind correction, and your personal margin requirements.
5
You load the airplane with four passengers near max gross. The CG is at the aft limit. How does this affect your stall characteristics and aircraft controllability?
Connect CG position to pitch authority, stall behavior, and recovery margins — not just the numbers.
