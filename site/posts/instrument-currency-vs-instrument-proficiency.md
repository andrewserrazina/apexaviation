Here's a question worth sitting with: when did you last fly in actual IMC?

Not simulated. Not under the hood with a safety pilot. Actual instrument conditions — clouds, low visibility, the kind of flying your instrument rating was designed for.

For most instrument-rated pilots, the honest answer is: a while ago. And that gap between "legally current" and "actually ready" is one of the most underappreciated risks in general aviation.

## What Currency Actually Means

Under **14 CFR §61.57(c)**, you're instrument current if, within the preceding six months, you've logged:

- **6 instrument approaches**
- **Holding procedures and tasks**
- **Intercepting and tracking courses through the use of navigational systems**

That's it. Six approaches, some holds, some tracking. You can accomplish all of this under the hood in VFR conditions with a safety pilot and never touch a cloud.

The FAA sets a minimum. It doesn't define readiness.

## What Proficiency Actually Means

Proficiency is harder to define — which is exactly why it doesn't appear in the FARs. But any CFII who's done IPCs will tell you they see the same pattern: a pilot who is technically current but whose instrument scan has degraded, whose SA in the system is slower than it used to be, and who hesitates at decision points that should be automatic.

Currency keeps you legal. Proficiency keeps you safe.

The gap between the two grows fastest when pilots fly infrequently in actual IMC, use autopilot for most of their approaches, fly the same routes and the same approaches repeatedly, and avoid challenging weather situations rather than building competence in them.

None of those behaviors violate any regulation. All of them quietly erode the skill you built to earn your rating.

## The Six-Month Cliff

Currency has a hard edge: six months from your last qualifying experience. One day past that, and you can't act as pilot in command under IFR.

But proficiency degrades on a curve, not a cliff. A pilot who flew one ILS three months ago technically qualified for currency — but whether that pilot is ready for a real-world single-pilot IFR departure into a low-ceiling environment is a different question entirely.

The most honest instrument pilots treat the six-month clock as a reminder, not a qualification. If you've let it go four months without meaningful IFR work, you're probably not at the proficiency level you think you are.

## What an IPC Actually Tests

An Instrument Proficiency Check isn't just a box to check when you let currency lapse. It's a structured assessment across the full IFR task spectrum:

- Pre-flight planning and weather analysis
- Air traffic control procedures and system familiarity
- Flight by reference to instruments alone
- Navigation system usage — VOR, ILS, GPS, RNAV
- Instrument approaches — precision and non-precision
- Missed approach procedures
- Holding patterns
- Partial panel (failed instruments)
- Unusual attitude recovery
- Emergency procedures

A good IPC takes 90 minutes to two hours in the aircraft — or can be done in substantial part in a qualified simulator. It gives you an honest picture of where your skills are, not just whether you've hit the minimum log entries.

## Using a Simulator for Currency and Proficiency

Under **14 CFR §61.57(c)(2)**, you can maintain instrument currency entirely in an FAA-approved flight training device — including an AATD — without setting foot in an aircraft. The requirements are the same: six approaches, holds, tracking.

More importantly, a quality AATD gives you something aircraft-based currency flying often can't: the ability to practice in truly challenging conditions. You can fly a missed approach into a 200-foot ceiling. You can lose a vacuum pump on final. You can get vectored for an unexpected hold when you're already low on fuel. You can do it again, reset, and do it better.

That's proficiency training. And you can't safely do it in a real aircraft.

## The Honest Check

Before your next IFR flight, ask yourself:

- When did I last fly an approach to minimums in actual IMC?
- When did I last deal with an unexpected hold or reroute in the system?
- When did I last practice partial panel?
- If I lost the autopilot on an approach tonight, would I be ready?

If the answers make you pause, that's useful information. An IPC isn't a punishment — it's a reset. Most pilots come out of one feeling more confident than they went in.
