Austin is one of the best cities in the country to be a general aviation pilot. The metro area has multiple towered airports, several untowered fields with great IFR approaches, and enough terrain variation to make cross-country flying genuinely interesting.

If you're based in Central Texas — or thinking about it — here's a practical breakdown of the GA airports that matter.

## Austin Executive Airport (KEDC)

**Location:** Northeast Austin, roughly 15 miles from downtown  
**Tower:** Yes (part-time)  
**ILS/RNAV:** RNAV approaches, no ILS  
**Elevation:** 620 ft MSL  
**Best for:** Primary training base, local flying, touch-and-goes

KEDC is the heartbeat of Austin general aviation. It hosts multiple flight schools, a busy pattern on weekends, and sits just far enough from KAUS to avoid constant Class C traffic management. The field has good services — fuel, maintenance, tie-downs — and the northeast Austin location puts it within practical reach of Round Rock, Cedar Park, Pflugerville, and the entire corridor running up I-35.

The Runway 18/36 orientation means crosswinds are common during spring frontal passages — which is good for training if you're trying to build crosswind experience, and something to plan around if you're not.

One note: KEDC sits under the KAUS Class C outer area. Southbound departures will enter the Class C within a few miles. Know your clearance plan before you go.

## Georgetown Municipal Airport (KGTU)

**Location:** Georgetown, 30 miles north of downtown Austin  
**Tower:** No (CTAF 122.8)  
**ILS/RNAV:** VOR/DME, RNAV approaches  
**Elevation:** 790 ft MSL  
**Best for:** Instrument practice, cross-country fuel stop, low-traffic pattern work

Georgetown is underrated. It sits clear of the Class C, has a VOR on the field that makes it useful for traditional instrument work, and offers a relaxed traffic environment compared to KEDC. The city of Georgetown has grown significantly in recent years, which means more local pilots — but the airport still feels uncrowded compared to the metro fields.

For instrument students, KGTU is one of the best practice environments in the area. You can shoot RNAV approaches repeatedly without the frequency saturation of KAUS-adjacent traffic, and the VOR-A approach is worth knowing cold if you're building IMC skills.

## San Marcos Regional Airport (KHYI)

**Location:** San Marcos, 30 miles south of Austin  
**Tower:** Yes (part-time)  
**ILS/RNAV:** ILS 13, RNAV approaches  
**Elevation:** 597 ft MSL  
**Best for:** Instrument training, ILS practice, southern metro cross-countries

San Marcos is where serious instrument work gets done in Central Texas. The ILS runway 13 is a genuine precision approach to instrument minimums — 200 feet and a half mile — and the tower environment means you're practicing real ATC communication on every approach.

This is the home airport for Apex Aviation's FlightDesign CTSW, and the reason is exactly what's described above: ILS practice, a professional tower environment, and enough traffic to make things real without being overwhelming.

If you're building toward your instrument rating and you haven't shot the ILS 13 at KHYI yet, put it on your list.

## Austin-Bergstrom International (KAUS)

**Location:** Southeast Austin, 5 miles from downtown  
**Tower:** Yes (24-hour)  
**ILS/RNAV:** Multiple precision approaches  
**Class C:** Surface to 4,000 ft MSL within 5 NM; 1,200-4,000 ft MSL from 5-10 NM  
**Best for:** IFR cross-countries, airliner observation, realistic ATC environment

Most GA pilots don't base at KAUS — ramp fees and airliner traffic make it impractical for training — but flying into and out of KAUS is valuable experience for any pilot who wants to fly IFR in a complex environment.

Shooting the ILS 17L at KAUS on a busy afternoon, with Southwest Airlines on a two-mile final behind you, is a different experience than shooting the same approach at an untowered field. It's the right kind of difficult. If your instrument ticket is going to mean anything, you need to be able to operate in this environment.

For GA operations, KAUS has a dedicated general aviation ramp on the south side. Expect a handling fee. Expect professionalism from the controllers. Plan your taxi carefully — this is a large airport with complex taxiway geometry.

## Lago Vista Airport (RR6)

**Location:** Lago Vista, 30 miles northwest of Austin on Lake Travis  
**Tower:** No (CTAF 122.8)  
**ILS/RNAV:** RNAV approach  
**Elevation:** 1,253 ft MSL  
**Best for:** Scenic flying, weekend day trips, Lake Travis access

RR6 sits on the Highland Lakes chain northwest of Austin, with views of Lake Travis that make it one of the better "reasons to go flying" airports in the state. The RNAV approach has you descending toward the lake with the Hill Country rising around you.

The elevation and terrain make it a useful airport to understand when you're flying northwest of Austin — terrain awareness matters more here than at the flatland airports, and weather can set in quickly over the Balcones Escarpment just a few miles to the west.

## Burnett Municipal (BMQ)

**Location:** Burnet, 55 miles northwest of Austin  
**Tower:** No (CTAF 122.8)  
**ILS/RNAV:** RNAV approach  
**Elevation:** 1,284 ft MSL  
**Best for:** Cross-country building, Hill Country destination

Burnet is a classic short cross-country from any Austin-area field — long enough to practice cruise flight and destination planning, short enough to do in an afternoon. The Highland Lakes Recreational Area makes it a popular destination on VFR days.

Pay attention to Hill Country weather if you're going northwest of Austin, especially in spring. The Balcones Escarpment creates localized lifting and can produce isolated convection on afternoons that look benign on the forecast. The VFR into IMC risk is real — it's something we talk about explicitly in Apex ground school for exactly this reason.

## Taylor Regional Airport (2TE8)

**Location:** Taylor, 35 miles northeast of Austin  
**Tower:** No  
**ILS/RNAV:** RNAV approach  
**Elevation:** 561 ft MSL  
**Best for:** Northeast metro cross-countries, Samsung corridor flying

Taylor's profile has risen with Samsung's semiconductor campus going in just south of town. If you're flying northeast of Austin — toward Taylor, Georgetown, or Hutto — knowing 2TE8 as an alternate and fuel stop is worth doing.

## Choosing Your Home Airport

If you're a student pilot or building hours in the Austin area, your choice of home airport matters more than most people think.

KEDC gives you the most infrastructure and flight school options. KHYI gives you the best instrument training environment south of KAUS. KGTU gives you the most relaxed practice environment north of town.

The best pilots in any metro know all of these airports — their approaches, their traffic patterns, their tower personalities, and their weather tendencies. Cross-country flying in Central Texas means knowing which airport fits the mission, not just which one is closest.
