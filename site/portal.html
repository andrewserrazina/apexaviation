10 Questions DPEs Love to Ask

The private pilot oral exam questions worth mastering before checkride day — with model answers, DPE insight, and success tips for each.

Why This Guide Exists

Every private pilot applicant eventually asks the same question: "what is my DPE actually going to ask me?" There's no official list — and there shouldn't be, since a real oral exam is a scenario-based conversation, not a fixed script. But after years of training pilots and talking with examiners, certain questions come up again and again, because they sit at the center of what a DPE is genuinely trying to find out: are you the kind of pilot who can be trusted to make good decisions alone in an airplane?

This guide walks through ten of those questions — not just the "correct answer," but why DPEs ask each one, and a specific tip for how to actually land it on exam day. We've also included a set of harder "Apex Challenge" questions to test how deep your understanding really goes, plus a practical Checkride Success Checklist you can use the week of your exam.

This is a preview, not the whole picture. It's built from the same question style, format, and standard used in our complete Private Pilot Checkride Prep Pack — 256 fully developed questions across every ACS domain. If these ten feel useful, the full guide is a natural next step, and we'll tell you more about it at the end.

Checkride Success Tip — How to Use This Guide

Don't just read the model answers. Cover them, say your own answer out loud first, then compare. Speaking your answer is what actually builds checkride-ready recall — reading alone doesn't.

The 10 Questions

1. What are the eligibility requirements to apply for a Private Pilot Certificate?

Model Answer

You must be at least 17 years old, be able to read, speak, write, and understand English, and hold at least a third-class medical certificate (or qualify under BasicMed for certain operations), along with the required aeronautical experience and endorsements.

Why DPEs Ask This

This is almost always the opening question of an oral exam. It's low-difficulty content, which is exactly why examiners use it — a clean, complete answer sets a confident tone before the harder material starts.

Checkride Success Tip

Don't stop at age and medical. A complete answer includes the English-language requirement and endorsements too — completeness is what separates a strong opening from an average one.

2. What is the process under 14 CFR 91.213 when equipment is inoperative?

Model Answer

First check if the aircraft has an approved Minimum Equipment List (MEL) — if so, follow it. If there's no MEL, determine whether the inoperative item is required by the type certification, by 91.205, by an Airworthiness Directive, or is otherwise flight-critical; if not required by any of those, it may be removed or deactivated and placarded inoperative, or the flight may proceed.

Why DPEs Ask This

This is one of the most commonly tested multi-step regulatory questions on the exam, precisely because it's easy to memorize the pieces but hard to walk through them in the correct order under pressure.

Checkride Success Tip

Always ask the MEL question first, out loud, before anything else. Skipping straight to "is it required equipment" is the single most common way applicants stumble on this question.

3. What's the difference between the entry requirements for Class B and Class C airspace?

Model Answer

Class B requires an explicit ATC clearance to enter — not just radio contact. Class C requires only established two-way radio communication with ATC, which is a lower bar than an actual clearance.

Why DPEs Ask This

Examiners love this pairing because the two requirements sound similar but aren't, and mixing them up under pressure is a real, documented cause of pilot deviations — not just a trivia gap.

Checkride Success Tip

Say the exact phrase every time: "clearance" for Class B, "established two-way communication" for Class C. Precision here signals genuine understanding, not memorization.

4. How do you decode a METAR?

Model Answer

In order: station identifier, date/time, wind, visibility, weather/obstructions, sky condition, temperature/dew point, altimeter setting, and remarks.

Why DPEs Ask This

This tests a skill you'll use before nearly every real flight you ever take — a DPE wants to see genuine fluency, decoded live and cold, not a memorized definition of what a METAR is.

Checkride Success Tip

Don't skip the remarks section to get to the "important" parts faster. That's exactly where an examiner likes to plant the detail that actually matters.

5. What factors increase takeoff distance?

Model Answer

High density altitude, higher aircraft weight, a tailwind component, an uphill or soft/contaminated runway surface, and reduced flap settings below the optimal takeoff setting.

Why DPEs Ask This

A DPE is checking whether you can name a genuinely complete list under pressure, not just the two or three factors that come to mind first.

Checkride Success Tip

Combine factors mentally before a real departure, not just for the exam: a hot day, an uphill runway, and a slight tailwind together compound fast into a real performance problem.

6. What does the IMSAFE checklist stand for, and how do you use it?

Model Answer

Illness, Medication, Stress, Alcohol, Fatigue, Emotion — a personal fitness-to-fly self-assessment performed honestly before every flight.

Why DPEs Ask This

This is rarely just a definitional question. Examiners often ask you to apply it to yourself, today, out loud — they're testing genuine self-awareness, not acronym recall.

Checkride Success Tip

If you're honestly a little nervous about the checkride itself, say so, and explain how you've managed it. Real self-awareness scores far better than a rehearsed "I'm totally fine."

7. What are the "four C's" of lost procedures?

Model Answer

Climb (for a better view and radio/GPS reception), Communicate (contact ATC or Flight Service for help), Confess (clearly state you're unsure of your position), and Comply (follow the guidance you're given).

Why DPEs Ask This

This question tests whether you'd actually use the process in a real moment of uncertainty — including the psychologically harder step of admitting you're lost to ATC.

Checkride Success Tip

There's no professional downside to confessing uncertainty to a controller. They deal with this constantly and are there to help, not to judge — say this with genuine confidence.

8. What is your procedure for an engine failure immediately after takeoff?

Model Answer

Establish best glide (or the appropriate pitch attitude), land essentially straight ahead within a narrow range of turn, and avoid attempting to turn back to the runway at low altitude unless specifically trained and confident in that maneuver at sufficient altitude.

Why DPEs Ask This

This is the classic "impossible turn" question. Examiners are listening for whether your default instinct prioritizes a safe, controlled landing over a risky return to the runway.

Checkride Success Tip

Know your specific aircraft's and airport's actual numbers, not just the general warning. A vague answer here reads as memorized; a specific one reads as genuinely internalized.

9. Walk me through what happens on your checkride day, start to finish.

Model Answer

Arrival and document review, followed by the oral exam, then the flight portion, then a debrief where the examiner either issues a temporary certificate or a Notice of Disapproval with a path to retest on the deficient area only.

Why DPEs Ask This

This question checks whether checkride day feels genuinely familiar to you — anxiety often comes from uncertainty about the process itself, not the content.

Checkride Success Tip

Push for the full sequence, including the debrief. Applicants who jump straight to the flight portion often forget the document review and debrief exist.

10. What is the ACS, and how is it different from the older Practical Test Standards?

Model Answer

The ACS is the current FAA standard defining the required Knowledge, Risk Management, and Skill for each Task within an Area of Operation. Unlike the older PTS, which focused primarily on skill performance, the ACS explicitly integrates knowledge and risk-management reasoning into every task — which is why the oral exam tests judgment, not just facts.

Why DPEs Ask This

This is arguably the single highest-value question in this entire guide. A DPE listening for a surface-level "it's basically the same thing with a new name" answer is listening for whether you actually understand the standard you're being measured against.

Checkride Success Tip

Don't let this one pass with a shallow answer. Explain specifically what changed — the explicit Risk Management element — and you'll sound like someone who read the ACS, not just heard of it.

Apex Challenge Questions

Five harder, next-level questions — the kind that separate a passing answer from a genuinely impressive one. Try each out loud before you look anything up. If you find yourself unsure on more than one or two, that's useful information: it's exactly the kind of gap our complete Prep Pack is built to close.

  1. Why does an alternator failure give a pilot more time to respond than a total electrical failure would — and how should that change your load-shedding priorities at night versus during the day?
  2. A friend offers to pay you $200 to fly them to a business meeting two states away — a trip you weren't already planning to take. Can you legally accept the payment? What if you were already planning to fly there that day regardless?
  3. Your Knowledge Test Report shows two missed questions, both coded to airspace-related ACS Tasks. Your DPE asks what you did about it before today. What's a genuinely strong answer?
  4. You calculate today's takeoff distance at 1,850 feet on a 2,200-foot runway, on a hot afternoon with calm wind. The book numbers say you'll make it. Would you actually make this flight, and what margin would you want?
  5. How does CG location affect stall speed and controllability — and why might a DPE ask this alongside a stability question rather than a weight-and-balance question?

Checkride Success Checklist

What to Bring

  • Government-issued photo ID
  • Student pilot certificate / medical certificate
  • Completed and signed IACRA application (Form 8710-1)
  • Logbook with all required endorsements
  • Knowledge Test Report
  • Aircraft documents — ARROW (Airworthiness certificate, Registration, Radio license if applicable, Operating limitations, Weight and balance data)
  • Aircraft maintenance logs (or evidence of required inspections)
  • Charts, plotter, E6B, and your completed cross-country flight plan
  • Examiner's fee, in the payment method they specify in advance

Day-Before Checklist

  • Confirm weather and NOTAMs for your planned checkride route one more time
  • Verify every document above is physically gathered in one place, not scattered
  • Review your cross-country flight plan and be ready to explain every number in it
  • Get a full night's sleep — treat this as seriously as you would before a flight lesson
  • Focus on your weakest, already-identified areas rather than cramming brand-new material

Morning-Of Checklist

  • Eat a real meal — low blood sugar undermines focus exactly when you need it most
  • Arrive early, not just on time, to avoid a rushed, stressed start
  • Do a final honest IMSAFE check on yourself
  • Remind yourself: your instructor endorsed you because they believe you're ready. Trust that assessment.

During the Exam

  • If you don't know something, say so clearly and explain how you'd find the answer — this is a strength, not a weakness
  • Speak in complete, organized answers rather than fragments
  • A single missed question is recoverable — don't let it derail your composure for the rest of the exam
  • Box breathing (inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4) if you feel your nerves spike
