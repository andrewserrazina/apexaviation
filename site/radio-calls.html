Before You Call
Have the current ATIS/AWOS, know your airport diagram, and brief your expected taxi route.

Pro Tip
If you don't understand a taxi instruction, STOP and ask for progressive taxi. Even experienced pilots do this.

Why Students Freeze on the Mic

Most students don't struggle with flying.
They struggle with talking while flying.

Radio calls feel like a foreign language — until you know the pattern.

There's no official script for a radio call — but there is a pattern every controller and every pilot expects. Once you internalize it, you stop translating in your head and start talking naturally, even under pressure.

🎙

You freeze the second you key the mic.

Not knowing what to say first is the single biggest source of radio anxiety — and it's completely fixable with a repeatable structure.

📋

You don't know what to read back.

Missing a required readback is one of the most common — and most avoidable — mistakes student pilots make on the radio.

🛫

Non-towered airports feel like a free-for-all.

Without a tower telling you what to do, knowing exactly what to say (and when) is what actually keeps everyone separated.

Sample — Tower Communications
Example Takeoff Call
"Barnes Tower, Warrior 12345, holding short runway 20, ready for departure."
Example Landing Call
"Barnes Tower, Warrior 12345, 5 mile final runway 20."
Readback Instructions
Always read back runway assignments, hold short instructions, and landing clearances. A correct readback helps catch mistakes before they become problems.
